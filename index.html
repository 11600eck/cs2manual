Aim Fundamentals

🎯
Crosshair Placement
Always keep your crosshair at head height and pre-aimed at where enemies will appear. If your crosshair is on the floor, you're losing every duel before it starts. Develop this as a reflex — it's the single highest-value habit at this tier.
🖱️
Sensitivity & Setup
Aim for 400–800 DPI × 1.5–2.5 in-game sensitivity. Lower is generally better for precision. Raw input ON, mouse acceleration OFF. You don't need expensive gear — consistency beats hardware every time.
⏸️
Burst, Don't Spray
CS2's recoil is punishing for beginners. Instead of holding down spray, fire 2–4 round bursts and release. At range, single shots only. Learn the AK-47 spray pattern when you're ready — but burst first.
🧰
Aim Training
Spend 10–15 minutes in aim_botz or the CS2 aim maps before queuing. Work on flick shots at close range, then graduate to tracking. Consistency comes before speed.
Pro tip — the first bullet
The very first bullet of any weapon is perfectly accurate when standing still. Use this — stop, shoot, move. Don't fire while running unless it's a shotgun.
02

Movement

01
Counter-Strafe
Tap the opposite key before shooting. If moving right, tap A briefly before firing. This instantly kills your momentum and makes your shot accurate. This is the most important mechanical skill in CS2.
02
Walk Don't Run
Hold Shift to walk silently. Running makes noise heard across the map. When rotating or holding an angle, walk. Save running for rotations when the enemy knows where you are.
03
Don't Jump-Peek
Jumping while shooting makes you wildly inaccurate. Never jump into a fight unless you're clearing a boost or escaping. Crouching briefly to peek angles can be useful but stay unpredictable.
04
Peek Corners Wide
When peeking an angle, swing out wide rather than tight. A wide peek gives you more time to see and react to the enemy before they see your full body. This is called "shoulder peeking" at advanced levels.

01

Utility — The Real Separator

💨
Smokes
Learn 3–5 smokes per map on the maps you play. Prioritize smokes that cut off CT rotations (e.g. jungle on Mirage, CT and B apps on Inferno). A good smoke execute wins rounds without firing a shot.
💥
Flashbangs
Pop-flashes are the key skill here — a flash that comes from behind cover and gives you no time to look away. Learn one or two pop-flashes per site entry. Communicate "flashing" before you throw.
🔥
Molotovs / Incendiaries
These are position denial tools. Use them to force enemies out of common spots (CT on Mirage, boost positions, B site corners). On defense, delay a push. On offense, clear common angles before committing.
💣
HE Grenades
Best used to combo with molotovs or soften clustered enemies. Don't waste an HE on a full-health player in cover — save it for low-health targets or to finish a fire damage victim.
One smoke at a time
Don't try to memorize 20 smokes at once. Learn 3 on one site, play 50 matches with them, then add 3 more. Muscle memory beats knowledge you can't execute under pressure.
02

Game Sense & Reads

01
Use the Radar
Glance at your minimap every 5–10 seconds. Where are your teammates? Where did you last hear enemies? Information is everything — the radar shows you what you know. Build a picture.
02
Count Enemies
When a teammate calls a kill, that's one fewer enemy on the map. Track the count. If two are confirmed A and you're on B with four teammates, you can likely push. Numbers advantage is free information.
03
Timing Awareness
Learn how long rotations take on your maps. If a CT pushes mid on Mirage, their B site is temporarily weak. The 15-second rule — after a kill in CT territory, rotate within 15 seconds or the advantage is gone.
04
Don't Trade Yourself
After a teammate gets killed, resist the urge to rush in. Your enemy is watching the same angle. Re-angle, use utility, or wait. A traded kill doesn't help — a traded kill where you survive does.
05
Play for the Objective
Planting the bomb matters even when you're losing. A planted bomb forces CTs to retake — even if you die, your teammates may finish the round. Never give away a bomb plant opportunity.
03

Team Roles — Know Yours

🔰
Entry Fragger
First through the door. Your job is to trade yourself for information and space — not necessarily frags. Use utility to enter, be aggressive, and accept that you may die. You open the round for your team.
🤝
Support
Flash your entry fragger in. Throw the smoke. Hold their flank. Your stats will look modest, but good support players win more rounds than any individual fragger. Communication and utility are your weapons.
🧭
In-Game Leader (IGL)
Call the strats. Where are we going? Are we defaulting or executing? A bad call made with confidence beats no call at all. Step up if nobody else is leading — even basic shotcalling lifts your team.
🔫
AWPer
Play to take duels you should win. Hold long angles, get a pick, then re-position. Never get caught in a dueling war with a rifler up close. A dead AWP is $4,750 gone and lost map control.
🛡️
Lurker
Create chaos on the opposite side of the main execute. Kill rotating CTs, take map control, or stall. A good lurk at the right time can win a round by itself. Bad lurking just leaves your team short-handed.
04

Communication That Actually Helps

The 3-second rule for callouts
"Two A long, low health" — that's a perfect callout. Location, count, health. Done in one second. Don't narrate your death. Don't blame teammates. Call the info your team needs to win the round.
01
Call Before You Die
The moment you spot an enemy, call it out before the fight. "One B window" the second you see them — not after you're already dead. Dead men give the most valuable intel.
02
Say "Flashing" Before You Flash
This one is simple and almost nobody does it. One word saves your teammate from getting blinded. Develop the habit in every practice session.
03
Stay Positive
Tilted teams lose. If someone makes a mistake, say nothing or say something useful. "It's fine, next round" is more valuable than any tactical call when your team's morale is cracking.

01

Macro Strategy & Default Play

🗂️
Default vs Execute
A default is a slow, information-gathering play — spread the map, collect intel, then commit late. An execute is a coordinated site push with full utility. Knowing which to call based on what you know about enemy positions is the highest-level T-side decision.
⏱️
Timing Control
Early aggression baits CT rotations and exposes where they are. Early mid-fight on Mirage opens B or A depending on how they respond. Map control and timing go hand-in-hand. Use information before committing bodies.
🔄
Fake Executes
Throw utility on one site, then go to the other. A convincing fake forces CTs to rotate — and rotating players can't be holding angles on both sites. Fakes work once per half. Use them strategically, not randomly.
🏰
CT Setups
Static setups get read after one half. Mix aggressive plays with passive holds. Push mid early some rounds, passive hold others. Force T-side to gather new information every round rather than execute off memory.
02

Advanced Mechanics

01
Jiggle Peeking
Briefly expose yourself at an angle to bait a shot or gather information without fully committing. If the enemy fires, you know they're there and roughly where. Then take the duel on your terms — counter-strafe, peek, fire.
02
One-Way Smokes
Certain smoke positions let you see through the bottom while they cannot see you. These are map-specific and require workshop practice to land consistently. On Inferno CT, Mirage B site, and others, they create free kills.
03
Crouch-Peeking Duels
Crouching while peeking changes your hitbox in a way that throws off pre-aimed crosshairs. Combined with a counter-strafe, you become very hard to hit in the first frame of a duel. High-risk but highly effective in the right spots.
04
Spray Transfer
After killing one enemy while spraying, transfer the recoil pattern to the next target without resetting. This requires knowing the spray pattern deeply — but a clean two-tap spray transfer on a double-peeking pair is devastating.
05
Bunnyhop (BHop)
Maintaining speed through consecutive jumps lets you rotate faster while being harder to hit. CS2's bhop is harder than CS:GO's — you must jump right before landing. Useful for rotations, not for peeking.
03

The Mental Game

🧘
Tilt Management
At this level, tilt is your biggest enemy — not the enemy team. One bad round doesn't lose a match. Develop a reset routine: take a breath, focus only on what you can control right now. Past rounds don't exist.
📈
Process Over Outcome
Stop playing to win rating. Play to make the correct decision every round. If you make 25 correct decisions and lose because of teammates or luck, you still improved. Rating follows process.
🔁
Adaptation Mid-Half
If the same site execute keeps failing, the enemy has adapted. Switch site, change timing, fake. Insanity is running the same execute a fifth time expecting different results. Read what's working and commit to it.
😴
Session Discipline
High-level play demands full cognitive bandwidth. Stop at 2–3 losses in a row, or after 3 hours regardless. Fatigued play locks in bad habits. Quality sessions over marathon grinding — always.
The plateau trap
Most players stuck at a rating ceiling are playing the same way they always have. Identify your most common mistake across 10 demos — one specific, repeating mistake — and fix that one thing. Fixing one deep habit is worth more than grinding 100 ranked games.
04

Advanced Economy Management

01
Force vs Eco — The Real Calculation
Don't force because it "feels right." Force when: you can win the round AND save your economy if you lose, OR when the round is winnable with the buy advantage you'll create. Blind forces just hand the enemy money and weapons.
02
Drop Calls
If your AWPer dies and loses the rifle, coordinate drops immediately at the next buy. Priorities: AWPer gets money first, then riflers, then entry players. Know your team's cash before the buy phase ends.
03
Eco Guns That Punch Up
On a hard eco: Deagle + Armor is your best chance to take a duel and grab a rifle. Save the MP9/MAC-10 for anti-eco rounds. Shotguns in tight corridors (Inferno B, Nuke) can legitimately swing eco rounds.

Watch pros on your maps
Find a pro player who plays your role on the maps you play. Watch their POV demos — not highlights, full matches. Pay attention to where they position, what utility they use and when, and how they move before fights. Copy the decision-making, not the flashy plays.
